Overview

When I'm thinking about interacting with public authorities, from requesting particular documents in order to complete an application form to a simple signature authenticity, I can‚Äôt help but feel overwhelmed by the lengthy procedures, waiting in lines, and broken communication channels.

In September 2021, our client reached out to the software agency I was working with a vision to improve citizens' everyday communication with public authorities in Bahrain. Communication between both parties had been a known challenge for many years, as it was almost entirely based on physical interaction, followed by bureaucratic procedures and clunky service experience.

Onebox would be a completely new product that our team could start building from scratch, having lots of autonomy over product decisions, design direction, and code architecture. This was a fantastic opportunity to join this project as it allowed me to spend the first couple of months on product discovery and research, something quite rare in an agency environment.

At that time, I was also leading a team of 7 designers in my company. My knowledge around discovery became much more robust, which inspired me to share lots of new learnings with my team and re-imagine and improve our discovery/research approach on our future projects in our organization.

product discovery Why are we building this product, and who are we building it for?

My principle while approaching UX research was to focus on the problem space as much as possible. I wanted conversations about deliverables to be redirected to the root cause, always investigating the underlying challenge or opportunity. As it was important to understand why we are building this product, who it is for, what challenges people in Bahrain are facing, and how they overcome those problems today, I wrote a proposal suggesting to our clients how to kick off the discovery phase.

I teamed up with my PM, and together, through a series of stakeholder interviews and impact mapping workshops, we were able to understand more about the goal of the project and its related actors. During that time, I learned that our goal should be to assist our client's team by identifying the key opportunities that would essentially turn into a coherent solution, enabling them to:

  • Get government support and establish a strong partnership with public authorities and small/medium enterprises in Bahrain.
  • Expand services beyond Bahrain and partner with Egypt Post, starting in Q3 of 2022.

Our client aimed to build a large ecosystem. Institutions such as banks, SMEs, telcos, etc., would mainly interact with a back-office, B2B product (later to be developed). Onebox application targeted:

As a team, we prioritized citizens as our primary user group. By the end of the interviews, a few learnings stood out:

Lack of security
  • Expats reach out to external agencies for managing their interactions with authorities, but they are afraid, as their sensitive information are handled by 3rd parties.

  • Citizens want to request specific documents but are not sure who to contact

    •      1st iteration Figuring out the navigation and IA

    Based on research, I needed to come up with an approach that is more centered around what is essential for the user, keeping in mind how to bring value to Onebox partners as well:

    • Filter what's important for the user to read
    • Inform users whether a message requires immediate action
    • Let public authorities differentiate themselves by offering a more personalized experience (custom branding, a news feed, services, etc.)

    My team and I held multiple UX workshops to brainstorm about the app's navigation and basic information architecture. Among many, we selected the concept below and decided it would be the building block of the first round of prototyping.

    Sketches of the 1st iteration
    • The concept behind the Home screen was that it would act as an action center. Users would be able to view all important messages that require an action to be performed (payment, signature, etc.) and the list of senders offering their services.
    • The purpose of the Inbox screen was to archive all unimportant messages, like bank statements, data usage from the user's tariff, etc. Still, users could filter the messages by Bills, Contracts, and Statements, making it easier to orient.
    • The partner detail screen would aggregate all messages received by that particular sender. Its primary purpose was to allow Batelco's partners to apply custom branding on their ‚Äúuniverse‚Äù and display all their available services.
    • On the message detail, users would be able to read the message they have received, perform any required action by the sender and set a reminder.
    The POC's user flows. The main goal was to settle the navigation and come up with a simplified version of setting custom reminders.     testing First round of usability testing

    Clearly, the concept above was simplified and was missing a lot of details and functionality, but it was sufficient to continue learning and building on top of it.

    I performed 6 rounds of usability testing, intending to bring any navigation challenges to the surface and other significant issues that would help our team shift focus and iterate.

    Tasks
  • Take a look at the home screen and describe what is shown.
  • Find Batelco Invoice for February 2022.
  • Set a reminder on 20th February at 6pm.
  • Find a list of messages that have been saved for reminder, including the newly added one.
    •      testing Results

    All tasks were completed successfully, apart from the last one, which no one could accomplish. Even though the success rate was reasonable, the time spent on the tasks was significantly more than expected. Alongside participants' hesitations and comments, I validated my hypothesis that navigation is clumsy and that we need to return to the whiteboard and rethink the user flow.

    Major findings during usability testing     testing Planning next steps

    I gathered all findings, and after a presentation to our stakeholders, they agreed to focus on the following suggested improvements:

    Visual simplicity and IA
  • Improve the visual hierarchy and contrast of the design. The current design is in complete white hues, which doesn't help in differentiating between sections and other content
  • Simplify the information architecture. Inbox screen seemed to be redundant and a lot of screens resembled one another.
  • Redesign the Home screen in a way that it looks more like an action center, with a focus on whether there are any pending actions required by the user or not.
    • Senders-centric design Introduce custom branding on the sender detail screen, that will both benefit the user as well as the sender:
  • Senders would be able to distinguish themselves through custom branding, having the ablility to offer their services, improve their communication with their customers or adopt new ones.
  • Users on the other hand will easily understand with which sender they are interacting with.
    •      2nd iteration Home screen iterations Some of the early concepts of the home screen

    During the research, I learned that citizens are expected to receive a maximum of 2-3 important messages per month. The rest would be statements, receipts, or messages with informational content. We defined important messages as the ones that require attention or immediate action by the user.

    I started exploring many different concepts (some of them shown above). The first concept (categorized content) was moving in the right direction - it was focused on senders rather than displaying a raw list of messages. Eventually, we didn't go with this idea, as there was no way to inform users about the type of messages that required their attention. The rest of the concepts were discarded as well. They were too content-heavy, and we aimed to simplify the home screen rather than overcomplicate it.

    In the end, I came up with an idea that focused more on displaying content as an overview, still keeping senders in the spotlight.

    • The initial design of the bottom menu and the inbox were removed.
    • The home screen acted as a crossroad to different ‚Äúuniverses‚Äù, and navigation had been flattened and simplified.
    • The content felt more personalized and covered both the happy scenario and the pending state.
    • Badges were replaced by smaller red dots. Users were expected to receive multiple unimportant messages per day, and we didn't want scary large numbers to feel overwhelming.
    Chosen concept     2nd iteration Branded partner universes Some of the early concepts of the home screen

    One of our client's business goals was to establish a strong partnership with public authorities, allowing them to distinguish themselves through custom branding, provided services, and news feed. I quickly dove into exploring various ideas on how to achieve the above.

    Some ideas were around making the partner universe feel more like a contact profile with shortcuts to different sections. The news feed was presented as a banner to draw attention, and quick actions (message, call, services) were shown at the top for direct interaction with the authority.

    After discussions with stakeholders, we felt the ‚Äúprofile‚Äù concepts would deviate from the original idea - reading important messages - so I didn't continue exploring that direction. We've also discussed that the concept of a news feed as a banner would remind a lot of advertising, which conflicted with the app's value proposition (ad and spam-free content). By the end of this exploration phase, we agreed to the following:

    • Focus on messages and introduce a menu at the bottom to switch to other views.
    • Remove the repetitive avatar shown at the start of the message review and replace it with visuals that would indicate the type of messages, along with their current state (pending/resolved), in case an action was required.
    Chosen concept     2nd iteration Presenting structured data

    Structured data were important information for the user that a sender would manually (or sometimes automatically) insert into the sent message, like due dates, the total amount to be paid, etc.

    The difficulty in visualizing those data was that it was unclear what possible use cases we would be facing. Due to time limitations, our stakeholders couldn't provide us with a list of messages to get more context and understand things like the number of actions that will be shown to the user, the number of metadata that will be displayed, etc. So I organized a workshop with my PM, and together we brainstormed and tried to answer those questions based on our experience interacting with various authorities. We split them into 5 categories: Education, Health, Banks, Government offices, and Telcos.

    Snippet of our brainstorming session

    In the end, the following steps were clear: I needed to design a solution flexible enough for any kind of content, as structured data could be up to 5-6 and actions up to 2-3. At that time, in the current implementation, attachments were fixed at the bottom. All the structured data and actions would take up lots of space, making it impossible to read the message on smaller devices. So I started experimenting with different layouts and interaction patterns until I came up with a solution closer to our goal.

    Snippet of our brainstorming session
    • I introduced a bottom sheet, which would have 3 states (default, collapsed and full screen).
    • When opening the message detail screen, the drawer will be on its default state.
    • On scroll (or by dragging the drawer down), the drawer will go on collapse state.
    • By dragging the drawer all the way up, it would go to full screen state, where all information about the message will be shown
    • By tapping on the ‚Äúx‚Äù icon (or by dragging all the way down), the drawer would go back to the default state.
    Chosen concept     Preparing for RTL optimization

    During the research phase, I learned that the majority of the GCC population consists of expats. However, the vision was still to distribute the app to countries where Arabic is the primary language. So when designing, it was important to think about optimizing the app for Arabic and the UI's directionality to match the language's reading behavior. After discussions with engineers, the directionality of the UI could be handled mainly by them. On the design front, there needed to be extra focus on typography and icons.

    Typography
  • Arabic is not case sensitive, which is why the Arabic font size, when its English version is in uppercase, needed to be increased to give that emphasis that's missing.
  • Letter spacing may break letters apart, showing unnecessary spacing, so it had to be kept to 0.
    •  Uncertainty
  • Icons that imply direction needed to be flipped.
  • There was no need to change icons that indicate right-hand usage, such as a search icon.
    •      pre-release testing Second round of usability testing

    Before our first release, I performed another round of usability testing, talking to 5 participants in total for 30 minutes each. The goal was to understand the following:

    • Whether there are any issues in navigating within the app.
    • Participants' ability to perform specific actions (paying a bill, setting a reminder)
    • Participants' understanding of the screen after an important message has been resolved, but still having unread messages received by that sender.

    Overall, there was a positive outcome compared to the previous version:

    • The home screen was more straightforward, and people understood it was a crossroad to messages grouped by sender.
    • We resolved the navigation and IA issues we previously faced.
    • Many of the features people identified (search, user avatar, services, call) were understood and found useful.
